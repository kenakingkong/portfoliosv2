Published 2026-07-01

This spring, I wanted to learn what it took to publish a successful children’s book. I quickly discovered it depends on what “successful” means: having a positive impact on child development, popularity among children, or winning awards and attracting publishers.

This piece focuses on what I learned taking a formal introductory course, hearing published authors speak about their books and reading research papers on child development and how my findings will guide my next steps in creating a children’s book of my own.

## Introduction to making children’s books

The 8-session class¹ I took offered a look into the ins and outs of the children’s book-making process and **what might make an award-winning book**. The instructor, illustrator and author Diana Toledano, taught the basics like types of books, book structure, terms, and character development - all through examples of award-winning children’s literature and short exercises.

The course covered key concepts like memorable characters, plot vs. story, hooks and intrigue, “kill your darlings”, and the importance of peer critiques and adult appeal. However, the course didn’t cover research-backed literary devices or concrete storytelling techniques.

## Techniques for approaching difficult topics

I attended a talk² on approaching difficult topics in picture books by authors Sharon Fujimoto-Johnson, Sheila Hackbarth, and Nikoo Yahyazadeh, who explained the techniques they used to connect with their readers when writing books about dangerous situations and fear.

Yahyazadeh said children understood the gravity of war **through the emotions of friends** who were torn apart, Hackbarth explained that including **hidden imagery** helps build confidence among slower readers, and Fujimoto-Johnson explained that children enjoyed **funky, imperfect illustrations** over perfect depictions.

This talk sent me on a journey to find more storytelling techniques grounded in child psychology.

## The impact of picture books on child development

I followed up with public research articles and studies done on children’s picture and story books. The consensus was that children’s books are important for **building language development and reading comprehension, critical thinking and moral reasoning, and empathy**.⁵ The magnitude of developmental impact depends on how the adult (parent/teacher) engages the children through reading and storytelling, like **discussing what was read** and making predictions.

There were specific techniques, like the use of predictive rhyming to help children remember character names⁶, the balance of heavy illustration vs. heavy text for imagination and expressive storytelling⁷, and the potential negative impact of fantasy on learning and understanding messages⁸. The measured impact varies per age group.

The techniques were interesting, and which ones an author reaches for depends on the effect they want to have on the reader. But I was surprised to see that the biggest impact on development came from conversations during a shared reading experience.

## Total books in home vs educational attainment

In contrast to the research on development, economist Steven Levitt generalized that a child’s future was most impacted by **scholarly culture**. The referenced study⁴ indicated that **having more books**, any books, in the home was more indicative of a child’s future education level than the parents’ education or income level.

However, this study did not reference the impact of picture books on developing other skills like language comprehension, it only measured success through final educational attainment.

For me, the takeaway is that books need to appeal to the adults who actually buy them. A book can’t impact a child unless it’s put in front of them first. And before a parent can buy a book, a publisher has to print it, which is its own battle.

## Publishers and creating books that sell

It’s difficult to find an agent or get published without enduring countless rejections. Additionally, the large publishing houses have absorbed many independent publishers, and media conglomerates have acquired many publishing houses. These trends shift the **child from reader to consumer** and the market for books that sell.⁹

Toledano instructed us that if the first page is not interesting, the publisher will not read the rest of the book. She also mentioned that entering contests and awards was a good way to get your name out there. Hackbarth resorted to self-publishing her book and Yahyazadeh had to negotiate key parts of the illustrations and story that were deemed too serious for the audience.

The author must maintain a sales- and market-driven mindset when creating their story in order to get published, win awards, and sell.

## Conclusion

There are a few ways a children's book can be “successful”, but I realized none of them matter if the book isn't bought by a parent and read to a child. A book can’t develop someone it never reaches.

The first step is to read recent award-winners to understand what is selling and why, choose my topic and target age group, and dig deeper into research-backed storytelling and illustration techniques for children. Then I’ll write the book, first page first, since that’s as far as a publisher will read if I get it wrong.
